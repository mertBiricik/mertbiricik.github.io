Demystifying Cybersecurity: A Deep Dive into CompTIA Security+ Fundamentals

Cybersecurity is a dynamic and critical field, constantly evolving to combat new threats. For anyone looking to establish a strong foundation in this domain, the CompTIA Security+ certification is an excellent starting point. This post will break down key concepts from the Security+ curriculum, covering essential controls, core security principles, threat actors, common vulnerabilities, and more.

Let's dive in!

1.1 Understanding Cybersecurity Controls

Cybersecurity controls are safeguards or countermeasures to avoid, detect, counteract, or minimize security risks to physical property, information, or any other asset. They can be categorized in several ways:

  • Technical Controls: These are implemented using systems and technology. Examples include operating system controls, firewalls that filter network traffic, and antivirus software designed to detect and remove malicious programs.
  • Managerial Controls (Administrative Controls): These are policies and procedures established by management to govern security. This includes creating clear security policies and defining standard operating procedures (SOPs) that employees must follow.
  • Operational Controls: These controls are implemented by people and focus on the day-to-day security activities. Examples include the presence of security guards, regular security awareness training programs for employees, and incident response procedures.
  • Physical Controls: These controls limit physical access to facilities and assets. This can involve guard shacks at entry points, fences, locks on doors, and badge readers to control entry to restricted areas.

Control Types: How Controls Function

Beyond their category, controls also have different functions in how they mitigate risk:

  • Preventive: These controls aim to stop an incident from happening in the first place. Think of a strong firewall preventing unauthorized access â€“ it's the "you shall not pass" of cybersecurity.
  • Deterrent: Designed to discourage an intrusion attempt. The threat of consequences, like demotion for policy violations, or visible warning signs (e.g., "CCTV in operation"), serve as deterrents.
  • Detective: These controls identify and log an attempt or an ongoing incident. Intrusion detection systems (IDS) or security logs are prime examples, alerting you to suspicious activity.
  • Corrective: Applied after an event has occurred to minimize damage or restore systems. The goal is minimal downtime, such as restoring data from backups after a ransomware attack.
  • Compensating: Used when a primary control cannot be implemented or is impractical. It's a control that achieves the same security objective through alternative means.
  • Directive: These controls guide a subject towards security compliance. While often considered "weak" on their own, they direct users to follow security best practices.

1.2 The CIA Triad: The Pillars of Information Security

The CIA Triad (sometimes called the AIC Triad) represents the three fundamental principles of information security:

  1. Confidentiality: Ensuring that information is accessible only to those authorized to have access.

    • Access Controls: Restricting who can view or modify data.
    • Encryption: Transforming data into a coded format to prevent unauthorized reading.

  2. Integrity: Maintaining the accuracy and completeness of data, ensuring it has not been altered or destroyed in an unauthorized manner.

    • Hashing: Creating a unique, fixed-size digital fingerprint of data. If the data changes, the hash changes.
    • Digital Signatures: When a hash is encrypted with a private key, it becomes a digital signature, providing both integrity and authenticity.
    • Certificates: Digital documents that bind a public key to an identity, used to verify integrity and authenticity.
    • Non-Repudiation: The inability to deny having performed an action or made a statement.

  3. Availability: Ensuring that authorized users have timely and uninterrupted access to information and resources.

    • Redundancy: Duplicating critical components or systems to ensure continued operation if one fails.
    • Fault Tolerance: The ability of a system to continue operating without interruption when one or more of its components fail.
    • Patching: Applying updates to software to fix bugs and security vulnerabilities, ensuring system stability and availability.

1.2.1 Non-Repudiation: Proving What Was Said

Non-repudiation means you cannot deny what you have said or done. It provides undeniable proof of origin and integrity.

  • Proof of Integrity: Verifies that data has not been changed since it was created or sent. Data remains accurate and consistent. Hashes are crucial here, acting as a data fingerprint. However, a hash only validates the data's integrity, not its origin.
  • Proof of Origin: Proves the source of a message or data. This is achieved through authentication mechanisms.

To ensure a signature is not fake (non-repudiation), digital signatures are used. A digital signature is essentially the encrypted hash of a message using a private key, which can then be decrypted with the corresponding public key to verify authenticity and integrity.

1.2.2 AAA Framework: Identity and Access Management

The AAA framework is a cornerstone of identity and access management:

  • Identification: The process by which a user claims an identity (e.g., by providing a username).
  • Authentication: The process of verifying that the claimed identity is genuine (e.g., by providing a password, biometric, or token). Certificate Authorities (CAs) play a role in issuing and managing digital certificates used for authentication.
  • Authorization: Determining what an authenticated user is permitted to do or access. Abstraction (like using groups) is often used to streamline administration of permissions.
  • Accounting: Tracking user activities, including what resources they accessed, when, and for how long. This provides an audit trail.

1.2.3 Gap Analysis: Finding the Gaps in Security

Gap analysis is a process used to compare the current state of an organization's security posture against a desired state or a recognized standard (like ISO/IEC 27001). It involves:

  • Evaluating People and Processes: Assessing the human and procedural elements of security.
  • Comparing Weaknesses and Solutions: Identifying security weaknesses and proposing solutions to compensate for them.
  • Analysis and Report: The outcome is a detailed analysis and report outlining the identified gaps and recommendations for improvement.

1.2.4 Zero Trust: Never Trust, Always Verify

Zero Trust is a security model based on the principle of "never trust, always verify." It assumes that no user or device, whether inside or outside the network perimeter, should be trusted by default.

  • Different Functional Planes of Network: Zero Trust often conceptualizes networks in terms of a data plane (where data flows) and a control plane (where policies are enforced).
  • Adaptive Identity: Identity verification is continuous and adaptive, considering context like device health, location, and behavior.
  • Threat Scope Reduction: By segmenting networks and enforcing granular access, the potential scope of a breach is significantly reduced.
  • Policy-Driven Access Control: Access decisions are strictly based on policies, not network location.
  • Security Zones: Networks are divided into micro-segments or security zones.
  • Policy Enforcement Point (PEP): The point where access policies are enforced, often at the individual resource level.

1.2.5 Physical Security: Protecting the Perimeter

Physical security measures are crucial for protecting physical assets and limiting unauthorized access to facilities.

  • Access Control Vestibules (Man Traps): Small rooms with two doors, where the first must close before the second opens, trapping an unauthorized individual.
  • CCTV (Closed-Circuit Television): Surveillance cameras for monitoring and recording activity.
  • Security Guards: Human presence for monitoring, patrolling, and responding to incidents.
  • Sensors: Various types of sensors (e.g., motion, door/window, vibration) to detect intrusions.
  • Infrared Cameras: Used for night vision and detecting heat signatures.

1.2.7 Deception and Disruption: Trapping Attackers

These techniques are used to mislead or disrupt attackers, gathering intelligence on their methods.

  • Honeypot: A decoy system designed to attract and trap attackers, making them believe it's a legitimate target. It's often assumed the attacker is a machine (automated attack).
  • Honeynet: A network of honeypots, providing a more realistic and complex target for attackers.
  • Honey Files: Decoy files placed on systems to detect unauthorized access.
  • Virtual Bear Trap: A metaphorical term for a honeypot, designed to ensnare attackers.
  • Honeytoken: A fake credential (e.g., API key, database entry) placed in a system to detect when it's accessed by an unauthorized party.

1.3 Change Management: Controlling the Flow of Change

Change management is a formal process for managing any alteration to an IT system or environment. Its goal is to avoid downtime, confusion, and mistakes.

  • Formal Process: Any change needs to follow a defined, formal process.
  • Rollback Procedures: Plans for reverting a change if it causes unforeseen problems.
  • Clear Policies: Well-documented guidelines for how changes are initiated, approved, and implemented.
  • Change Request Form: A standardized form to document the details of a proposed change, including:
    • Purpose: Why the change is needed.
    • Scope: What systems or areas will be affected.
    • Schedule: When the change will occur.
    • Systems and Impact: Detailed analysis of affected systems and potential consequences.
    • Risk Associated with the Change: Identification and assessment of potential risks.
  • Approval: Changes require formal approval from relevant stakeholders.
  • End-User Acceptance: Ensuring that the change is acceptable to the end-users who will be affected.
  • Ownership: Clearly defined responsibilities for the change.
  • Managers/Stakeholders: Individuals who will be impacted by the change or have a vested interest in its outcome.
  • Impact Analysis: Assessing the potential effects of a change across the organization.
  • Risk Value: Quantifying the risk associated with the change.
  • Lihon on Far Reaching: (Likely a typo, possibly "Likelihood of far-reaching impact") Assessing how widely the change might affect systems or users.
  • Sandbox Testing: Testing the change in an isolated, non-production environment.
  • Backout Plan: A detailed plan for reversing a change if it fails or causes issues.
  • Standard Operating Procedure (SOP): Detailed, written instructions to achieve uniformity of the performance of a specific function.

1.3.1 Technical Change Management

This focuses on the technical aspects of managing changes.

  • Allow/Deny List: Managing access based on explicit permissions or restrictions.
  • Downtime: Minimizing service interruptions during changes.
  • Legacy Applications: Special considerations for older systems that may be fragile or difficult to modify.
  • Documentation: Thoroughly documenting all changes.
  • Change Management Protocol: The established set of rules and procedures for managing changes.

1.4 Public Key Infrastructure (PKI)

PKI is a framework that enables the secure exchange of information using public key cryptography. It involves:

  • Policies, Procedures, Hardware, Software, People: A comprehensive system encompassing all these elements.
  • Digital Certificates: Used to create, distribute, manage, store, and revoke digital certificates.

Symmetric Encryption

  • Uses a single, shared secret key for both encryption and decryption. It's fast but key distribution can be a challenge.

Asymmetric Encryption (Public Key Cryptography)

  • Uses two mathematically related keys: a public key and a private key.
  • Data encrypted with the public key can only be decrypted with the corresponding private key, and vice-versa.
  • Relies on complex mathematical problems involving large prime numbers and randomization.
  • Key Escrow: A system where a copy of a private key is held by a third party, often for recovery purposes.

1.4.1 Encryption: Protecting Data at Rest and in Transit

Encryption is the process of converting information or data into a code to prevent unauthorized access.

  • Disk Encryption: Tools like BitLocker and VeraCrypt encrypt entire disk drives.
  • Key Stretching/Key Strengthening: Techniques that make a weak key stronger by increasing the computational cost of brute-force attacks, adding additional overhead for attackers.

1.4.2 Key Exchange

The process by which cryptographic keys are securely exchanged between two parties.

1.4.3 Encryption Technologies

Various hardware and software solutions support encryption:

  • Trusted Platform Module (TPM): A secure cryptoprocessor that stores cryptographic keys and protects them from malicious software.
  • Hardware Security Module (HSM): A physical computing device that safeguards and manages digital keys for strong authentication and provides cryptoprocessing.
  • **Key Management System (KMS): A system for managing cryptographic keys throughout their lifecycle.
  • Secure Enclave: A separate, isolated processor within a device (e.g., a smartphone) that has its own boot ROM and monitors the boot process. It provides real-time memory encryption and often includes a true random number generator for strong cryptographic operations.

1.4.4 Obfuscation: Hiding Information

Obfuscation techniques aim to make information difficult to understand or interpret, but it's not true security.

  • Steganography: Hiding information in plain sight, often by embedding it within another file, such as an image (the "covertext").
  • Concealed Writing: A general term for hidden messages.
  • Tokenization: Replacing sensitive data with a non-sensitive placeholder (a "token").
  • Near-Field Communication (NFC): A short-range wireless technology that can be used for data transfer, but also has security implications if not properly secured.
  • Data Masking: Obscuring specific data elements within a dataset to protect sensitive information while maintaining its usability for testing or analysis.

1.4.5 Hashing & Digital Signatures: Integrity and Authenticity

  • Hashing: Creates a one-way function where data cannot be recovered from its hash. Good hash functions should not have collisions (different inputs producing the same output). MD5 is an example of a hash function that is now deprecated due to collision vulnerabilities.
  • Salted Hashes: Adding a random string (salt) to a password before hashing it, making rainbow table attacks (precomputed hash tables) ineffective.

1.4.6 Blockchain Technology: Distributed Trust

  • Distributed Ledger: A decentralized database shared and synchronized across multiple sites, institutions, or geographies.
  • Everyone Has It: Each participant in the network has a copy of the entire ledger, ensuring transparency and immutability.

1.4.7 Certificates: Root of Trust

  • Root of Trust: A highly trusted authority (often a Certificate Authority) that serves as the foundation for trust in a PKI.
  • Certificate Authorities (CAs): Trusted third parties that issue and manage digital certificates.
  • Key Revocation: The process of invalidating a digital certificate before its expiration date.
  • Online Certificate Status Protocol (OCSP): A protocol used to obtain the revocation status of a digital certificate in real-time.

2.1 Threat Actors: Who Are They and What Do They Want?

Understanding threat actors involves identifying who they are, their motivations, and their capabilities.

  • Internal/External: Threat actors can originate from within an organization (insiders) or from outside.
  • Motivation:
    • Data Exfiltration: Stealing sensitive data.
    • Espionage: Gaining secret information, often for state or corporate advantage.
    • Service Disruption: Causing outages or hindering operations.
    • Blackmail: Extorting money or favors.
    • Financial Gain: Direct monetary profit (e.g., ransomware).
    • Beliefs/Ethical: Driven by ideological or ethical convictions (e.g., hacktivists).
    • Revenge: Seeking retribution.
    • Disruption and Chaos: Causing general disorder.
    • War: State-sponsored cyber warfare.

Types of Threat Actors:

  • External Entities:
    • National States: Nation-states engage in constant attacks with massive resources, often associated with Advanced Persistent Threats (APTs).
    • Unskilled Attackers: Individuals with limited technical knowledge, often using readily available tools. They can be both internal and external.
  • Hacktivist: Motivated by philosophical or political beliefs. They can also be internal. Their actions often include DoS attacks, website defacement, or releasing private documents. Funding may be limited.
  • Insider Entities: Individuals within an organization. They have access to internal resources and often possess medium-level sophistication.
  • Organized Crime: Almost always external, driven by financial gain. They are highly sophisticated, often employing tactics like ransomware.
  • Shadow IT: Employees or departments working around official IT policies, building their own infrastructure, which can create security vulnerabilities.
  • Going Rogue: An individual acting independently against organizational policies or norms.

2.2 Common Threat Vectors: How Attacks Happen

Threat vectors are the paths or methods attackers use to gain unauthorized access to systems or networks.

  • Message-Based Vectors:
    • Phishing: Deceptive emails or messages designed to trick recipients into revealing sensitive information or performing actions.
    • SMS (Short Message Service): Used for smishing attacks.
  • File-Based Vectors: Malicious code embedded in files.
  • Voice Call Vectors:
    • Vishing: Phishing conducted over the phone.
    • War Dialing: Automatically dialing phone numbers to find open modems or systems.
    • Call Tampering: Intercepting or altering phone calls.
    • Spam over IP: Unsolicited messages sent over Voice over IP (VoIP) systems.
  • Removable Device Vectors: Malware spread via USB drives or other portable media.
  • Vulnerable Software Vectors: Exploiting weaknesses in software.
    • Client-Based: Exploiting vulnerabilities in client-side applications.
    • Agentless: Attacks that don't require installing an agent on the target system.
  • Unsupported Systems Vectors: Exploiting vulnerabilities in outdated or unpatched operating systems and applications.
  • Unsecure Network Vectors:
    • Open Service Ports: Unnecessary open ports that can be exploited.
    • Firewall Rules: Misconfigured or weak firewall rules.
    • Default Credentials: Using default usernames and passwords that are easily guessed.
  • Supply Chain Vectors: Attacks targeting vulnerabilities in the supply chain, often through third-party vendors like Managed Service Providers (MSPs).

2.2.1 Phishing: The Art of Deception

Phishing is a common social engineering technique that uses tricks and misdirection to manipulate individuals.

  • Email Phishing: The most common form, using deceptive emails.
  • Typosquatting: Registering domain names that are slight misspellings of legitimate ones to trick users.
  • Pretexting: Creating a fabricated scenario (pretext) to gain trust and obtain information.
  • Vishing: Phishing conducted over voice calls (phone).
  • Smishing: Phishing conducted via SMS messages.

2.2.2 Impersonation

  • Pretext: A fabricated scenario used to gain trust and obtain information, often a key component of impersonation.

2.2.3 Watering Hole Attack

  • An attack where a cyberattacker compromises a website frequently visited by a target group, hoping to infect members of that group. Often countered by a layered defense strategy.

2.2.4 Other Social Engineering Techniques

  • Misinformation/Disinformation: Spreading false or misleading information to manipulate individuals.

2.3 Memory Injections: Exploiting System Memory

Memory injection attacks involve injecting malicious code directly into a process's memory space.

  • Malware Runs in Memory: Malware often operates directly in memory to evade detection.
  • Memory Forensics: Analyzing memory dumps to detect and investigate malicious activity.
  • DLLs (Dynamic Link Libraries): Malware can inject itself into legitimate DLLs or load malicious ones.
  • Threads: Malware can create or hijack threads within a process.
  • Buffers: Memory buffers can be exploited.
  • Management Functions: Malware can interfere with memory management functions.
  • Malware Hiding: Malware can either hide itself within an existing process or create a new, malicious process.

2.3.1 Buffer Overflows

  • Overwriting Buffer of Memory: Occurs when a program attempts to write more data into a fixed-size memory buffer than it can hold, overflowing into adjacent memory locations.
  • Bounds Checking: A defense mechanism that checks if data being written to a buffer will exceed its allocated size.
  • Repeatable: Buffer overflows are often repeatable, meaning they can be reliably exploited.

2.3.2 Race Conditions

  • When Two Events Happen at the Same Time: Occur when the outcome of a process depends on the sequence or timing of other uncontrollable events.
  • Time-of-Check to Time-of-Use (TOCTOU) Attack: A type of race condition where a system's state changes between the time it is checked and the time it is used, leading to a vulnerability.

2.3.3 Malicious Update

  • Trusted Sources: Attackers can compromise trusted update mechanisms to deliver malicious updates.
  • Backups: Regular backups are crucial for recovery from malicious updates.

2.3.4 Operating Systems Security

  • Regular Updates: Operating systems should be updated regularly to patch vulnerabilities.
  • Patch Tuesday: Microsoft's monthly release of security updates.

2.3.5 SQL Injection

  • Bad Programming: A common web vulnerability caused by insecure coding practices.
  • SQL Injection: Occurs when an attacker inserts malicious SQL code into input fields, which is then executed by the database.

2.3.6 Cross-Site Scripting (XSS)

  • Takes Advantage of Trust: XSS attacks exploit the trust a user has in a legitimate website.
  • Complex: XSS attacks can be complex and difficult to detect.
  • JavaScript-Based: Mostly relies on injecting malicious JavaScript code into web pages.
  • Non-Persistent (Reflected) XSS: The malicious script is reflected off the web server and executed in the user's browser. It's not stored on the server.
  • Persistent (Stored) XSS: The malicious script is permanently stored on the target servers (e.g., in a database) and delivered to users when they access the vulnerable page.

2.3.7 Hardware Vulnerabilities

Hardware can have vulnerabilities that are difficult to detect and patch.

  • OS Access: Some hardware may have an operating system but no direct user access, making them perfect entry points for attackers.
  • IoT (Internet of Things): IoT devices are often vulnerable due to weak security, default credentials, and lack of updates.
  • Firmware: Software embedded in hardware. Often, only the vendor can modify it.
  • End of Life (EOL)/End of Service Life (EOSL): When hardware or software reaches its EOL/EOSL, it no longer receives security updates, making it vulnerable.
  • Legacy Platforms: Older systems that are no longer supported or updated.

2.3.8 Virtualization Security

  • VM Escape Protection: Measures to prevent a virtual machine (VM) from breaking out of its virtualized environment and accessing the host system.
  • Resource Reuse: Securely managing the reuse of virtualized resources to prevent data leakage.

2.3.9 Cloud-Specific Vulnerabilities

Cloud environments introduce unique security challenges.

  • Authentication Bypass: Bypassing authentication mechanisms to gain unauthorized access.
  • Directory Traversal: Exploiting vulnerabilities to access files and directories outside of the intended web root.
  • Remote Code Execution: Executing arbitrary code on a remote server.
  • Out of Bounds Write: Writing data beyond the allocated memory buffer, potentially leading to code execution.

2.3.10 Supply Chain Vulnerabilities

  • Many Parts: Modern systems rely on many components from various suppliers, each introducing potential vulnerabilities.
  • Service Providers: Compromised service providers can introduce vulnerabilities into your systems.
  • Hardware Providers: Vulnerabilities in hardware components.
  • Software Providers: Vulnerabilities in third-party software.

2.3.11 Misconfiguration Vulnerabilities

  • Open Permissions: Files or directories with overly permissive access rights.
  • Unsecured Admin Accounts: Administrator accounts with weak passwords or no multi-factor authentication.
  • Insecure Protocols: Using outdated or unencrypted protocols (e.g., Telnet instead of SSH).
  • Default Settings: Leaving default configurations unchanged, which are often insecure.
  • Open Ports and Services: Unnecessary open ports or running services that can be exploited.

2.3.12 Mobile Device Vulnerabilities

  • Jailbreaking/Rooting: Modifying a mobile device's operating system to remove vendor restrictions, which can introduce security risks.
  • Sideloading: Installing applications from sources other than official app stores, increasing the risk of malware.

2.3.13 Zero-Day Vulnerabilities

  • Not Found Yet: A vulnerability that is unknown to the vendor and has no patch available. Attackers can exploit these before defenses are in place.

2.4 Malware: Malicious Software

Malware is a broad term for any software designed to cause damage, disrupt systems, or gain unauthorized access.

  • Ransomware: Malware that encrypts a victim's files and demands a ransom payment for decryption.

2.4.1 Viruses and Worms

  • Virus: Malicious code that attaches itself to legitimate programs and requires user interaction to spread. It can replicate and move between systems.
  • Fileless Virus: A type of malware that operates entirely in memory, leaving no traces on the hard drive.
  • Worm: Self-replicating malware that doesn't need user interaction to spread. It uses network vulnerabilities to propagate rapidly, often bypassing firewalls.

2.4.2 Spyware and Bloatware

  • Spyware: Malware designed to secretly gather information about a user's activities.
  • Bloatware: Unwanted software pre-installed on devices, often consuming resources and potentially posing security risks.

2.4.3 Other Malware Types

  • Keylogger: Records keystrokes to capture sensitive information like passwords.
  • Logic Bomb: Malicious code that lies dormant until a specific condition is met (e.g., a date, a user action).
  • Rootkit: A stealthy type of malware designed to hide its presence and other malicious processes on a system, often by modifying core operating system components.

2.4.4 Physical Attacks

  • Brute-Force: Repeatedly trying different combinations to guess passwords or unlock systems.
  • RFID Cloning: Copying the data from RFID (Radio-Frequency Identification) cards to create duplicates.
  • Environmental Attacks: Attacks that exploit environmental factors.
    • Turning Off Power: Causing a power outage to disrupt services.
    • HVAC (Heating, Ventilation, and Air Conditioning): Manipulating HVAC systems to damage equipment or disrupt operations.
    • Fire Suppression: Disabling fire suppression systems to cause damage.

2.4.5 Denial of Service (DoS)

  • Force Service to Fail: An attack designed to make a machine or network resource unavailable to its intended users.
  • Sometimes Friendly: Can sometimes be unintentional (e.g., a legitimate surge in traffic).
  • Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS): A DoS attack launched from multiple compromised computer systems (a "botnet") to overwhelm a target. Can be reflected and amplified using vulnerable servers.

2.4.6 DNS Attacks

  • DNS Spoofing/Poisoning: Injecting false DNS (Domain Name System) records into a DNS resolver's cache, redirecting users to malicious websites.
  • Domain Hijacking: Gaining unauthorized control over a domain name.
  • URL Hijacking: Redirecting users from a legitimate URL to a malicious one.
  • Typosquatting/Brandjacking: Registering domain names that are common misspellings or variations of legitimate brands to trick users.

2.4.7 Wireless Attacks

  • Wireless Deauthentication: Sending deauthentication frames to disconnect clients from a Wi-Fi network (802.11 standard).
  • 802.11ac: A Wi-Fi standard.
  • RF (Radio Frequency) Jamming: Deliberately interfering with radio signals to disrupt wireless communication.
  • Wireless Jamming: A general term for disrupting wireless signals, often by broadcasting random data.
  • Fox Hunting: The process of locating a hidden radio transmitter, often used in wireless security to find rogue access points.

2.4.8 On-Path Attack (Man-in-the-Middle)

  • ARP Spoofing/Poisoning: Manipulating ARP (Address Resolution Protocol) tables to redirect network traffic through an attacker's machine.
  • On-Path Browser/Man-in-the-Browser Attack: Malware that intercepts and modifies web traffic within the user's web browser.

2.4.9 Replay Attacks

  • Need Raw Network Data: Involve capturing legitimate data transmission and retransmitting it to impersonate a legitimate user or system.
  • Pass the Hash: An attack where an attacker steals a password hash and uses it to authenticate to a system without cracking the hash.
  • Session Hijacking (Sidejacking): Stealing a user's session ID to take over their active session.
  • E2E (End-to-End) Encryption: Crucial for preventing replay attacks by encrypting data from the source to the destination.

2.4.10 Malicious Code

  • General term for any code designed to perform unauthorized or harmful actions.

2.4.11 Application Attacks

  • Injection: Inserting malicious code into an application (e.g., SQL injection, command injection).
  • Replay: Replaying captured data to gain unauthorized access.
  • Privilege Escalation: Gaining higher-level permissions than initially authorized.
  • Cross-Site Request Forgery (CSRF): Tricking a web browser into executing an unwanted action on a trusted site where the user is authenticated.
  • Client-Server Codes: Exploiting vulnerabilities in the interaction between client and server applications.
  • Directory Traversal: Accessing files and directories outside of the intended web root.

2.4.12 Cryptographic Attacks

  • Hash Collision: Finding two different inputs that produce the same hash output.
  • Birthday Attack: A type of cryptographic attack that exploits the mathematics behind the birthday problem to find hash collisions.
  • Downgrade Attack: Forcing a system to use a weaker, less secure version of a protocol or algorithm.
  • SSL Stripping: An attack that downgrades an HTTPS connection to HTTP, allowing the attacker to intercept traffic.

2.4.13 Password Attacks

  • Should Be Hashed: Passwords should always be stored as hashes, not in plain text.
  • Spraying Attack: Attempting a single password against many accounts to avoid account lockouts.
  • Brute-Force: Systematically trying every possible combination of characters until the correct password is found.

2.4.14 Indicators of Compromise (IoC)

  • Any Event that Indicates an Intrusion: Evidence that a system or network has been compromised.
  • Account Lockout: Repeated failed login attempts leading to an account being locked.
  • Concurrent Session Usage: A single user account being logged in from multiple locations simultaneously.

Conclusion

This overview provides a foundational understanding of key cybersecurity concepts essential for the CompTIA Security+ exam and for anyone entering the field. From implementing robust controls and understanding the CIA Triad to identifying various threat actors and their attack vectors, a comprehensive approach to security is paramount in today's digital landscape. Continuous learning and vigilance are key to staying ahead of evolving threats.
